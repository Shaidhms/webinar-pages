002 / The Core Insight

Parallel Thinking vs. Adversarial Thinking

One shift that changes everything

Adversarial

How we think now

  • × Person A argues FOR the idea
  • × Person B argues AGAINST the idea
  • × Person C defends their territory
  • × The best debater wins
  • × Quiet voices are never heard
  • × Egos drive the outcome
vs

Parallel

Six Thinking Hats

  • ✓ Everyone wears the SAME hat together
  • ✓ Everyone explores facts together
  • ✓ Everyone explores risks together
  • ✓ Everyone generates ideas together
  • ✓ Every voice is equal under the hat
  • ✓ The process drives the outcome

The hats separate the THINKER from the THINKING.
You are not "being negative." You are wearing the Black Hat. This removes ego from the equation.

003 / The Six Hats

The Six Hats at a Glance

Everyone wears the same hat at the same time.

W

Facts & Data

Objective information only. No opinions, no interpretation. What do we know? What is missing?

Gut reaction. No justification needed. Everyone participates. 30 seconds per person max.

Critical judgment. What could go wrong? Be specific, not vague. Goal is protection, not destruction.

What could go wrong?

W Hat 1 of 6

White Hat

Objective information. No opinions, no interpretation.

Rules

  • Only verified, factual information
  • No opinions, arguments, or spin
  • Explicitly identify what data is MISSING
  • State sources: "According to the Q3 report..."
  • Distinguish verified facts from assumptions

Key Questions

  • What do we know for certain?
  • What data do we have? From where?
  • What information is missing?
  • How reliable is this information?
  • What would we need to know to decide?
Case Study

DuPont: Product Safety Reviews

DuPont trained 6,000+ employees. White Hat discipline forced teams to separate verified test data from 15-year-old assumptions. Meeting times fell by 75%.

■ Watch Out

People will inject opinions disguised as facts. Redirect: "That sounds like interpretation. What is the actual data?"

R Hat 2 of 6

Red Hat

Emotions, gut feelings. No justification required.

Rules

  • No need to explain or justify - this IS the point
  • 30 seconds per person maximum
  • Everyone participates. No opting out.
  • Feelings are data. Treat them seriously.
  • Keep it short: "I feel uneasy" is enough

Key Questions

  • What is your gut reaction right now?
  • How do you feel about this proposal?
  • If you had to bet your own money, would you?
  • Are you excited, worried, or neutral?
  • What does your intuition say?
Case Study

Siemens: Infrastructure Project

Data looked favorable, but Red Hat revealed deep unease from senior engineers. Extended analysis uncovered a regulatory risk. Project was restructured.

■ Watch Out

People will try to justify their feelings. Stop them: "Just the feeling. One sentence. No explanation."

Bk Hat 3 of 6

Black Hat

Critical judgment. What could go wrong?

Rules

  • Be SPECIFIC: "Supply chain risk in Q2" not "This might fail"
  • Use it during its turn ONLY - not as default mode
  • Goal is protection, not destruction
  • Always follow with Yellow or Green Hat
  • This is the MOST OVERUSED hat. Discipline it.

Key Questions

  • What could go wrong?
  • What are the risks we have not considered?
  • Does this fit our resources and timeline?
  • What is the worst-case scenario?
  • Are there regulatory or legal concerns?
Case Study

British Airways: Route Planning

Before Six Hats, Black Hat thinking killed every new proposal before exploration. Structuring it into a defined phase let creative ideas survive. Innovation rose.

■ Watch Out

This is the hat everyone wants to live in. It makes you look smart. CAP it. Move to Yellow or Green after.

Y Hat 4 of 6

Yellow Hat

Value, benefits, best-case scenarios.

Rules

  • Be specific: "Opens the 18-25 market" not "Could be good"
  • Explore benefits even for ideas you are skeptical of
  • This is HARDER than Black Hat. Push the room.
  • Every idea deserves Yellow Hat before Black Hat
  • Build the case before you stress-test it

Key Questions

  • What are the specific benefits?
  • What is the best-case outcome?
  • Who gains and how?
  • What value does this create that does not exist today?
  • Why could this work despite the challenges?
Case Study

Prudential: Micro-Insurance Product

A junior analyst's proposal would normally be shot down. Yellow Hat forced the room to explore value first: untapped segment, first-mover advantage. Product piloted.

■ Watch Out

Teams rush through Yellow to get to Black (criticism is easier). INSIST on thorough Yellow. Ask: "What else could be valuable?"

G Hat 5 of 6

Green Hat

New ideas, alternatives, lateral thinking.

Rules

  • NO judgment whatsoever - not even positive
  • Quantity over quality. Filter later.
  • "And also..." never "But..."
  • Use provocations: "What if we did the opposite?"
  • Silence is OK. Give people space to think.

Key Questions

  • What are entirely new approaches?
  • What if we removed all constraints?
  • What would a competitor do with this?
  • Can we combine two ideas into something new?
  • What is the most radical option available?
Case Study

NASA: Mission Engineering

Green Hat sessions separate idea generation from evaluation. Engineers trained to not self-censor. The non-obvious solution often saves the mission.

■ Watch Out

The moment someone says "That will not work," Green Hat is dead. Enforce ruthlessly: "Save that for Black Hat."

B Hat 6 of 6

Blue Hat

Meta-thinking. Managing the process itself.

Rules

  • Opens and closes EVERY session
  • Usually worn by the facilitator throughout
  • Decides which hat comes next
  • Summarizes after each hat phase
  • Calls out violations: "That is Black Hat. We are on Yellow."

Key Questions

  • What is our objective for this session?
  • What thinking is needed right now?
  • Which hat should we wear next?
  • Let me summarize what we have found so far.
  • What is our conclusion and what are the next steps?
Case Study

IBM: Strategic Planning

IBM trained dedicated Blue Hat facilitators. Multi-day planning meetings completed in hours. Dominant personalities neutralized by process. Rolled out globally.

■ Watch Out

Without a strong Blue Hat facilitator, the method collapses. Assign this role explicitly. It is not optional.

004 / Facilitation

How to Run a Session

Structure, timing, and facilitation rules

1

Assign a Blue Hat Facilitator

Manages process. Does NOT contribute opinions. Calls hat switches and summarizes.

2

Define the Topic (Blue Hat)

State the problem or decision clearly. Write it where everyone can see it. 1 minute.

3

Cycle Through the Hats

Use a sequence appropriate to your situation. 2-5 minutes per hat.

4

Enforce Hat Discipline

If someone is doing Black Hat during Yellow Hat, call it. "Save that for Black Hat."

5

Summarize After Each Hat

Blue Hat captures key outputs before switching. Creates a clear record.

6

Close with Blue Hat

Summarize findings. State decision. Define next steps. Assign owners.
